**     ॥आख्यातवादः॥**

** INTRODUCTION**

 I. RAGHUNATHA SIROMANI AND HIS WORKS

 Raghunatha Siromani, a disciple of Vasudeva sarvabhauma, and by far the most outstanding figure of the Bengal school of Navya-Nyaya, belonged to the fift;eenth century A.D.(1) He attained great prominence as a commentator of Tattvacintamani, an epoch-making work of Gangesa Upadhyaya.With the sole exception of Tattvacintamanyaloka of jayadeva misra,the earlier commentaries on Tattvacintamani wear casr into oblivion by Tattvacintamanididhiti of Raghunatha.It is really unfortunate that this work is not available in its entirety(2).Raghunatha also commented on Atmatattvaviveka of Udayana and Dravyakiranavaliprakasa, Gunakiranavaliprakasa and Nyayalilavatiprakasa of Vardhamana Upadhyaya. Gunakiranavaliprakasadidhiti(3) and Atmatattvavivekadidhiti(4) are now available in print.

 Raghunatha was not only a successful commentator-he was an eminent Philosopher in his own rihgt. while commenting on Gangesa,Raghunatha has criticised him on a number of occasions,and the alternative theories suggested by him won universal acceptance in course of time. The later Naiyayikas of Bengal made the Anumanadidhiti the main centre of their attention, and no less than fifty-two subcommentaries were written on it.(5) Even Gokulanatha Upadhyaya and vamsadhara Upadhyaya of the Mithila school, the traditional rival of the Bengal school,showed due respect to Raghunatha by commenting on Anumanadidhiti(6).But the original thinking of Raghunatha reaches its highest level in three independent works: Padarthatattvanirupana,Nanvada and Akhyatavada.Raghunatha also wrote a work relating to Dharmasastra, and it was called Malimlucaviveka.This shows that his interests were not confined to Navya-Nyaya alone.

 In Padarthatattvanirupana Raghunatha has Subjected the traditional Nyaya-Vaisesika metaphysics to a through examination. He has rejected a number of time-honured doctrines opposed by the Nyaya-Vaisesika school. This seems to be his best work.Nanvada deals with the meaning of the negative Praticles,and thus fills up a gap in the Sabdakhanda of Tattvacintamani. Anntated translations of Padarthatattvanirupana and Nanvada have been published in the Harvard Oriental series, and the reader is referred to these translations for more information about these texts.The contents of Akhyatavada, which deals with the meaning the verbal ending(akhyata),will be given in a later part of the introduction.

 It may be noted here that these inependent works of Raghunatha were

Commented on by a number of Naiyayikas,and some of them have also appeared in print.The reader is referred to Vange Navya-Nyaya Carca D.c.Bhattacharya for mor information about these commentaries on Padarthatattvanirupana and Nanvada.

 II . RAMABHADRA SARVABHAUMA AND HIS WORKS

 Ramabhadra sarvabhauma,the son of Janakinatha Cudamani,belonged in all probability to the first half of the sixteenth century A.D.(7) He was a prolific writer and a Great teacher.D.C.Bhattacharya has given a list of his works,which is as follows:

 (1)Nyayarahasya,

 (2)Kusumanjalikarikavyakhya,

 (3)Padarthatattvanirupanaprakasa,

 (4)Nanvadavyakhya,

 (5)Samasavada,

 (6)Sabdanityatvavada,

 (7)Suvarnataijasatvavada,

 (8)Gunarahasya,

 (9)Siddhantarahasya,

 (10)Siddhantasara and

 (11)Samayarahasya.(8)

 Of these,(1)is a commentary on the first four chapters of the Nyaya-Sutras,(2)is a commentary on the verses in Nyayakusumanjali of Udayana,(3)and(4)are commentaries on two tracts of Raghunatha.(5)-(8)are independent works,(9)and(10)are in all probability collection of small tracts,and(11)relates to Dharmasastra.(2)and(3) have appeared in print,and we are happy to add to this list one more work of ramabhadra,viz. his Akhyatavadavyakhya, which is a commentary on raghunatha's Akhyatavada.It seems that Bhattacharya did not come across any copy of this commentary.

 Ramabhadra was the teacher of Mathuranatha Tarkavagisa,Jagadisa Tarkalankara,Gaurikanta sarvabhauma and Jayarama Nyayapancanana.It seems that he was also the teacher of Srirama Tarkalankara,the father of Mathuranatha,and Harirama Tarkavagisa,the teacher of Gadadhara Bhattacarya cakravarti and Raghudeva Nyayalankara(9).It is a measure of success of Ramabhadra as a teacher that many among his illustrious students attained the rare academic stature of Jagadguru.

 We cannot undertake in the short compass of this introduction a detailed discussion of Ramabhadra's attainments as a commentator and philosopher.We reserve this task for the introduction to a critical edition of Ramabhadra's Nyayarahasya,which is in press.

 III. AKHYATAVADA

 The problem discussed in Akhyatavada is on the borderline of linguistics and semantics, and the arguments presented in it are sometimes dependent on the peculiarities of the sanskrit language.In Indian semantics,we come across many generalized theories about the meaning and syntactic structure of sentences,and the analysis of a sentence depends on the theory one chooses to adopt.For example,the Naiyayikas are in favour of Kartrmukhyakasabdabodha;i.e. they stipulate that in the verbal cognition generated by a sentence,what the word with nominative case ending means figures as the principal element.On the other hand,the Grammarians opt for Vyaparamukhyakasabdabodha;i.e. according to them, the verbal element in the sentence stands for the chief Qualificand featuring in the coginition generated by a sentence.The Bhatta and Prabhakara Mimamsakas also took part in this controversey,and played a significant role in it.

 As expected,the philosophers belonging to these rival schools could never agree on the meaning of verb,verbal ending and verbal suffix.But they observed some common rules,and this saved the dispute from degenerating into a series of monologues.Among these rules, we may mention the preference for parsimony(laghava)and the dictum that a word means a certained thing only if the latter canot be meant by anything else(ananyalabhyah sabdarthah).

 It may be noted here that verbs may be used in active voice(Kartrvacya)as well as passive voice(Karmavacya)to describe the same situation.We may use either 'Devadattah gramam gacchati'or 'Devadattena gamyate'when we want to say that Devadatta is going to a village.In the sanskrit language,the verbal endings(akhyata)employed in Kartrvacya and Karmavacya are known as Kartrakhyata and Karmakhyata.A proper theory about the meaning of akhyata must do full justice to both Kartrakhyata and Karmakhyata.Moreover, a theory of akhyata must be accompanied by a suitable doctrine about the meaning of verb(dhatu0,which,in its turn,must hold good for both transitive(akarmaka)verbs.

 The problem about the meaning of akhyata thus becomes a many-faced one,and several solutions to it havebeen suggested.The traditional view of the Nyaya school is that akhyata primarily means conscious effort(Akhyatasya yatno vacyah),and that the paradigmatic use of akhyata occurs in sentences where the word with nominative case ending denotes some conscious agent.The secondary meaning (laksyartha)of akhyata is operation conducive to a Particular result(anukulavyapara), which figures in sentences like "Ratho gacchati"(The chariot moves). In such cases,ihe nominative is something unconscious,and so,the question of conscious effort does not aries.The Bhatta Mimamsakas accuse the naiyayikas of prolixity (Kalpanagaurava),and maintain instead that the primary meaning of akhyata is

opertaion conducive to a particular result,since this holds good of all agents-conscious or otherwise.Mandana misra,a noted Bhatta mimamsaka, admits that in some cases the akhyata may mean operation (simpliciter anukulavyaparo vyaparamatram vakhyatarthah .For the vaiyakaranas,Kartrakhyata and Karmakhyata stand respectively for the nominative and accusative of the verb with which they are associated (Kartrkarmani lakaravacye).According to Tarani Misra,the author of Ratnakosa,the meaning of akhyata is the act of producing(bhavana).Finally,the Prabhakara Mimamsakas stress the fact that in the sanskrit language employment of akhyata is strictly governed by person,number and tense.That is why we can guess the person,number and tense associated with a conjugated verb,even when we are provided with only the latter.According to the Prabhakaras, a verbal stem means a particular opertation conducive to a particular result,and hence,it cannot give us any idea of number,person and tense;and as such,the later should costitute the meaning of akhyata.They also admit that in some cases(e.g.karmakhyata),the akhyata may also mean locushood(tatratatra tattatphalanukulatattadvyaparavisesa eva dhatvarthah,samkhyavartamanatvadikam kvacidasrayatvadikamapi vakhyatarthah).

 Gangesa has discussed these views in Akhyatavada and Dhatuvada(two chapters of Tattvacintamani)(9).Throughout the discussion he reiterates the nyaya view that sentences like "Devadattah pacati"(Devadatta is cooking)generates a verbal cognition of the form "Pakanukulakrtiman Devadattah"(Devadatta possessing an effort conducive to the cooking\[of Food\]).This claim is fotified by the interchangeability of the expressions "Devadattah pacati"and"Devadattah pakamkoroti-the later expression being a paraphrase(vivarana)of the former.According to the Naiyayikas,a verbal stem means a certain result or opertation(Phalavyaparau dhatvarthau),and as such,the verbal stem pac and the noun paka should mean the same thing.by the method of residue,the verbal ending-ti should means the same as the verb karoti.The verbal root kr,when conjugated,yields the form karoti,and kr stands for effort(krnasca yatnavacakatvam).It thus follows that the verbal ending primarily means effort.He also lays much stress on the primarily means effort.He also lays much stress on the principle of parsimony.The rival views are rejected as they fail to do justice to the verdict of experience,or else,indulge in theoretical prolixity.

 In his akhyatavada,Raghunatha has covered almost the same ground as Gangesa,but his style is remarkably concise,and in some respects,his treatment of the problem is superior to that of Gangesa.This may explain the fact that Raghunatha's Akhyatavada superseded the Akhyatavada of Gangesa(10).

 According to Raghunatha,the traditional Nyaya doctrine about the meaning of akhyata is not adequate.It is true that experience and reasoning support the view that akhyata primarily means effort.But the range of its secondary meaning is not exhausted by operation conducive to some result.There are a number of cases where the akhyata can never mean conscious effort or operation conducive to some

result.For example,in the sentence "Devadatto-janatio"(Devadatta knows),no conscious effort is indicated;nor is indicated on operation conducive to some result,for the act of knowing does not produce any tangible result in its object.The same holds good of cases like"Devadattah icchati"(Devadatta desires),"Devadattah svapiti"(Devadatta sleeps),"Devadatto vidyate"(Devadatta exists)etc.In these cases,the scope of the secondary meaning should be extended to locushood (asrayatva),i.e.the akhyata merely indicates that Devadatta is the locus of the act denoted by the verb.Again,in cases like "Ghato nasyati"(the pot perishes),the akhyata can on no account mean effort or operation conducive to a certain result.Raghunatha maintains that here the secondary meaning of the akhyata is counterpositiveness(Pratiyagitva);i.e.The Akhyata merely indicates that the pot is the counterpostive of Destruction.The discerning reader may note that Raghunatha partially admits the claim of the Prabhakara Mimamsakas.

 These views of Raghunatha did not remain Unchallenged.After Raghunatha,the Prabhakara school of Mimamsa began to dwindle away,and the fortune of the Bhatta school of Mimamsa was also on the decline.The Grammarian school,however,continued to exert its influence through able exponents like Kondabhatta and Nagesa.These Grammarians have tried to refute the view of Raghunatha,and cannot ignore their criticisms.For an excellent discussion of these criticisms,one may consult Philosophy of language by Pradip kumar Mazumdar(11).

 The Commentaries of these scholars,with the exception of those of Mathuranatha and Ramacandra,are yet to appear in print.

 Among these commentators,Ramakrsna and krsnadasa might be the senior contemporaries of Ramabhadra,and Bhavananda might be his junior contemporary.Mathuranatha and Jayarama were his students,and

viswanatha,Ramacandra,Rudra and Raghudeva were definitely junior to him.we cannot,however,settle the relative priority of Ramabhadra and Rupanarayana.

 Under these circumstances,it is hardly surprising that Ramabhadra rarely refers to the earlier commentaries on Akhyatavada.We come across such references on seven occasions(12)and on four occasions,Ramabhadra has criticized these views(13).The Commentary is breif,and attempts primarily at explaining Akhyatavada.On some occasions,he has also criticized the views of Raghunatha,(14) and this confirms to some extent the conjecture of D.C.Bhattacharya that Ramabhadra did not belonging to the group that follow Raghunatha.(15)This also shows that Ramabhadra was a commentatory of independent spirit.He has also noted some variants in Akhyatavada,and given his judgement about them.(16)

 Further research is essential for determining the indebtedners of Ramabhadra to the earlier commentators.Mathuranatha,being the student of Ramabhadra,sometimes agrees with the latter,(17) but his own commentary is much more extensive,and far outshines the commentary of his teacher.

 V. THE CRITICAL APPARATUS AND METHOD OF EDITING

 The Present edition of Akhyatavadavyakhya is based on a single manuscript found in a bundle that also contained manuscripts of Nyayarahasya and Padarthatattvanirupanavyakhya of Ramabhadra,Anviksikitattvavivarana of Janakinatha Cudamani,the Father of Ramabhadra and Ratnavalli of Raghava Pancanana,the elder brother of Ramabhadra.The discovery of Akhyatavadavyakhya and Ratnavalli was communicated by us in a paper presented at the 29th session of All-India Oriental Cinference.(18)

 We now give a brief description of the manuscript utilised by us(since the edition is based on this manuscript alone,we do not mention its beginning,end or colophon):

 The left margins of the folia are numbered correctly.The writing is neat and bold.The Manuscript contains several lacunae, which have been filled up by marginal insertions indicated by Kakapada-s.A portion of Raghunatha's Akhyatavada and explainatory comments are also found in the margins-all written in a different hand(similar features also characterize the manuscript of Padarthattvanirupanavyakhya

found in the same bundle .The name of the book does not appear on the margin-instead,we find 'srih' written on the left margins of the reverse side of the folia.A perforated on the left margins of the reverse side of the folia.A perforated square blank space has been left out at the centre of each folio.The edges have been damaged and discoloured,and some marginal insertions,comments and corrections have become too faint to be deciphered.Brown pigment has been used for erasures.

 The last folio is damaged at a vital place,making it difficult to decide whether the number 1540 refers to saka or vikramaera.It should,however be noted that the manuscsipt of Nyayarahasya found in the same bundle is dated sakabda 1540.More over,the date vikrama samvat 1540 is not consistent with the date of Ramabhadra.We are,therefore inclined to place the manuscript in sakabda 1540.

 A few remarks about the editioral method adopted here may not be out of place.Our text of Akhyatavada(printed in shorter passages)is based on two sources,viz.the printed text of Akhyatavada,with commentaries of Mathuranatha and Ramcandra,which was printed as an appendix to Tattvacintamani with several commentaries under the editorship of MM kamakhyanatha tarkavagisa,(calcutta:Asiatic society,1884-1901,vol.IV,pp.867-1009),and the fragment of Akhyatavada written on the margins of Akhyatavadavyakhya.these sources have been assigned the sigla A and M respectively.Readings supported by Ramabhadra have been preferred,but we have also consulted the readings accepted by Mathuranatha and Ramacandra.We have split up the text into passages,and in this,we have been guided by the remarks of Ramabhadra.We have also added some punctuations in Akhyayavada and Akhyatavadavyakhya.usual editorial practices have been adopted in editing the commentary.

 कथन्तर्हि "पचती"त्यादौ पाकजनकयत्नानुभव इति चेत्, यत्नाविनाभूतपाकादिना क्रियाविशेषकारणस्य (15) यत्नस्यानुमानात्। "पचति पाकविषयकयत्नवान्"(16) इति तात्पर्यविवरणम्। अन्यथा धर्मिणोऽपि(17) वाच्यतापत्तेः। अथैव वर्तमानत्वं यत्नस्य न प्रतीयेत, तस्यापदार्थत्वात्, अन्यत्र धात्वर्थक्रियायां स्वार्थव्यापारे वा लडादेर्वर्तमानत्वाद्यनुभावकत्वस्य व्युत्पन्नत्वाच्च(18)। न च पाकजनकव्यापारेण(19) पाकजनकवर्तमानयत्नानुमानं, यत्नविगमेऽपि व्यापारानुवृत्तेः। धर्मिविशेषनिष्ठता च यत्नस्य न स्यात्, तद्व्यधिकरणव्यापारस्यापि पाकजनकत्वात्। चैतन्याविनाभूतचैत्रत्वादिविशेषितेन तेन यत्नानुमानमिति चेत्, सत्यं,(20) चैत्रत्वाद्यप्रतीतावपि "शोभनं(21)पचती"त्यादौ शोभनादेः पाकयत्नत्त्वप्रीतेः(22) तत्राख्यातस्य जनकयत्ने लाक्षणिकत्वात्। मैवम्, जनकव्यापारमपेक्ष्य(23) लाघवेन(24) जनकयत्नस्यैव शक्यत्वात्। यत्नं विहाय जनकत्वमात्रे शक्तिरस्तु लाघवात्, तथा चाचेतनेऽपि प्रयोगो मुख्य एवेति चेन्न, अपचत्यपि पाकजनकादृष्टवति "पचती"ति प्रयोगापत्तेः। पाकजनकादृष्टजनककृतेश्च(25) न पाकजनकत्वं मानाभावात्। अतएव क्षित्यादेः कृत्यादिजन्यत्वे साध्ये तज्जनकादृष्टजनककृत्यादिना अर्थान्तरप्रसङ्गोऽपि प्रत्युक्तः। भावे वा तादृशकृतिनिवारणा(26) यादृष्टद्वारकजनकताया(27) स्त्वयाऽपि वाच्यत्वात्। यत्नमात्रं शक्यं विषयित्वं जनकत्वं वा संसर्गमर्यादया(28) भासत इति तु नव्याः॥3॥
